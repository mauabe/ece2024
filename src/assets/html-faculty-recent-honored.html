Henry Samueli Received the IEEE 2025 Medal of Honor

Henry Samueli is the 2025 IEEE Medal of Honor Laureate, recognized for developing communications products that have touched virtually every person in the world. He was presented with his medal at the IEEE Honors Ceremony on 24 April 2025 in Tokyo, Japan. An IEEE-Eta Kappa Nu Eminent Member, Samueli has given the $2M prize to fund a permanent endowment for Eta Kappa Nu. This permanent endowment will further his commitment to developing future leaders.

The IEEE Medal of Honor is the highest IEEE award, and one of the world\’s most prestigious technology-focused prizes. It was established in 1917 to recognize key advances in radio technologies by the greatest minds of our time for work that benefits humanity. Effective in 2025, IEEE increased the IEEE Medal of Honor monetary prize to $2 million because we are at an inflection point globally – where technology, engineering, and science have become integral to addressing global challenges such as climate change and positively moving our world forward. In the evaluation process, the significance of achievement, originality, impact on society, impact on the profession, publications and patents relating to the achievement, and quality of nomination are the top criteria.

Professors Ozcan, Zhang and Divsalar Elected to NAE

The National Academy of Engineering (NAE) is a non-profit, private organization established in 1964. It provides engineering leadership and advice to the nation, advancing the welfare and prosperity of the country. The NAE also conducts independent studies on important engineering and technology topics and comprises over 2,000 peer-elected members and international members. Its membership is considered one of the most prestigious recognitions reserved to the most accomplished engineers.

Aydogan Ozcan was elected to the National Academy of Engineers for his “contributions to mobile sensing and telepathology for medical diagnostics.” He holds the Volgenau Chair in Engineering Innovation at ECE, as well as a joint appointment at bioengineering. A pioneer of computational optics, Ozcan has developed new technologies with broad applications in biomedicine and health care that have helped increase access to advanced measurements in resource-limited areas. He has created high-resolution imaging and sensing techniques for use in mobile phones and other lower-cost portable devices and has developed a series of innovative microscopy techniques that use algorithms rather than traditional optical lenses for high-volume, high-resolution rapid imaging for biomedical sensing and diagnostics. Ozcan is also the associate director at the California NanoSystems Institute and is a professor with the Howard Hughes Medical Institute. For the past five years, he has been recognized as one of the world\’s most highly cited researchers by the international data and analytics firm Clarivate.

Lixia Zhang was elected to the National Academy of Engineers for her “development of internet protocols which significantly impacted internet quality and performance, and for leadership in setting standards.” She has been an influential researcher for more than three decades in the architecture, performance and security of the internet and its connected devices. Among her most notable achievements is the design of the Resource Reservation Protocol, or RSVP, a robust network signaling protocol used to manage network resources that has sustained internet growth for over two decades. Zhang was also an early contributor to the Internet Engineering Task Force, a global body that lays out the technical standards of the internet. She has also been one of the longest-serving members on the Internet Architecture Board.

Dariush Divsalar was elected to the National Academy of Engineers for his “theory and practice of channel codes that impact deep-space communications.” Professor Divsalar is a senior research scientist and fellow at NASA\'s Jet Propulsion Laboratory. He was elected to the academy for his “theory and practice of channel codes that impact deep-space communications.” Since 1978, he has worked at JPL on advanced deep-space communications systems for NASA missions, primarily through the development of coding and signal modulation techniques. His breakthroughs include extending the range and capacity of these systems and protecting the transmitted data\'s integrity. Some of his advances have also been incorporated into modern wireless communications applications. He is a triple Bruin engineer, having earned his master\'s, engineer and doctoral degrees from UCLA Samueli.

Chang, who holds the William Frederick Seyer Endowed Chair in Materials Electrochemistry, is also a professor of materials science and engineering and a member of the California NanoSystems Institute (CNSI) at UCLA. She focuses on the synthesis and patterning of multi-functional complex oxide films with tailored electronic, chemical, thermal, mechanical and biological properties. She leads the Electronic Materials Synthesis and Plasma Processing Lab, which has developed plasma etching at the atomic scale, enabling the creation of structured films. Chang is a fellow of the American Institute of Chemical Engineers and the American Vacuum Society. In 2018, she became the first woman to win the AVS Plasma Prize. Chang previously served as the director of Function Accelerated nanoMaterial Engineering — a major multidisciplinary research consortium headquartered at UCLA and supported by the Semiconductor Research Corporation and the Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency.
